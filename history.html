Built 1963 · An architectural first

The Continental
Sunset.

Originally christened The Continental Sunset, the building you know today as Sunset Penthouse was the first apartment in Los Angeles to clad itself in aluminum and glass — an architectural first that quietly announced a new way of living, just below the Sunset Strip.

A million-dollar idea, in blue.

Built and owned by the Appel-Slaten Development Co., the $1 million blue aluminum and glass structure introduced an architectural advancement Los Angeles had never seen in apartment construction. Four stories of curtain-wall innovation rose at the corner of De Longpre, one-half block south of Sunset Boulevard.

The building contained 51,000 square feet of floor space and a two-level underground garage. Twenty-four homes — one-, two-, and three-bedroom — many available custom-furnished or unfurnished, were spread across the floors above. Each apartment came with two-car parking, locked elevators, and an intercom system to the lobby.

“We feel we have incorporated the New York feeling for elegance with a highly contemporary California open design which should appeal to many professional people looking for the ultimate in town house apartment living.” — Slaten

Aluminum curtain. Floor-to-floor glass.

The press photograph from the building's debut shows what residents today still recognize: a clean horizontal banding of aluminum and glass, thin pilotis along the ground floor, and the unmistakable rhythm of mid-century West Hollywood.

The aluminum curtain wall — by then a familiar detail on New York office towers — had not yet appeared on a Los Angeles apartment house. The Continental Sunset was the first. Recreational facilities followed: a patio at grade and a sundeck on the roof, with the Hollywood Hills rising to the north.

Saturday and Sunday, twelve noon to seven.

Southern California's newest and most elegant luxury apartment building threw open its doors on a January weekend in 1963 — three furnished models open for inspection, beverages and hors d'oeuvres for everyone, and motion picture and TV personalities mingling under the new aluminum sky.

One-, two-, and three-bedroom apartments started at $260, with custom-furnished options for tenants who preferred to walk into a fully appointed home. The original handbill, lifted from the Sunday paper that weekend, still sets the tone of the place.
